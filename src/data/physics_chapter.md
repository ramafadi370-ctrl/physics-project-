# CHAPTER 23: ELECTRIC FIELDS

## Topic 1: Properties of Electric Charges

- There are two kinds of electric charge: positive and negative.
- Electrons carry negative charge; protons carry positive charge.
- Like charges repel each other; opposite charges attract each other.
- Electric charge is always conserved in an isolated system.
- Electrification by rubbing is due to charge transfer, not charge creation.
- A rubber rod rubbed with fur becomes negatively charged.
- A glass rod rubbed with silk becomes positively charged.
- A rubber rod (negative) and a glass rod (positive) attract each other.
- Two rubber rods (both negative) repel each other.

## Topic 2: Conservation and Quantization of Electric Charge

- When a glass rod is rubbed with silk, electrons transfer from glass to silk.
- The silk gains negative charge; the glass rod gains equal positive charge.
- Electric charge is quantized — it exists in discrete packets.
- The standard symbol for charge as a variable is q.
- Formula: q = ±Ne, where N is an integer and e is the fundamental unit of charge.
- The fundamental unit of charge: |e| = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
- Electron charge: q = −e. Proton charge: q = +e.
- Particle charges and masses (Table 23.1):
  - Electron: charge = −1.602176 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, mass = 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg
  - Proton: charge = +1.602176 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, mass = 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
  - Neutron: charge = 0, mass = 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg

## Topic 3: Conductors, Insulators, and Semiconductors

- Conductors: materials where some electrons (free electrons) can move freely.
- Free electrons are not bound to atoms.
- Examples of conductors: copper, aluminum, silver (metals).
- In a conductor, charge readily distributes over the entire surface.
- Insulators: materials where all electrons are bound to atoms.
- Charge cannot move freely in an insulator.
- Examples of insulators: glass, rubber, wood.
- Semiconductors have electrical properties between conductors and insulators.
- Examples of semiconductors: silicon, germanium.

## Topic 4: Charging by Induction

- Charging by induction requires no physical contact with the charging object.
- Steps of charging a metallic sphere by induction:
  1. Start with a neutral metallic sphere (equal positive and negative charges).
  2. Bring a negatively charged rod near (but not touching) the sphere.
  3. Electrons in the sphere redistribute — they move away from the rod.
  4. Ground the sphere — electrons can leave through the ground wire.
  5. Remove the ground wire — sphere now has a net positive charge (not uniform).
  6. Remove the rod — remaining electrons redistribute; sphere has uniform positive charge.
- A process similar to induction occurs in insulators: molecular charges rearrange (polarization).

## Topic 5: Coulomb's Law

- Charles Coulomb measured forces between small charged spheres.
- Coulomb's Law gives the electrical force between two stationary point charges.
- A point charge is a particle of zero size that carries electric charge.
- The electric force is:
  - Inversely proportional to the square of the separation r between charges.
  - Proportional to the product of the two charges q1 and q2.
  - Attractive for opposite charges; repulsive for like charges.
  - A conservative force.
- Coulomb's Law formula: Fe = ke × |q1| × |q2| / r²
- The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C).
- The Coulomb constant: ke = 8.9876 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
- ke = 1 / (4πε₀)
- Permittivity of free space: ε₀ = 8.8542 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
- The fundamental charge: e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (smallest unit, except quarks).
- 1 coulomb requires 6.24 × 10¹⁸ electrons or protons.
- Typical charges in practice are in the microcoulomb (μC) range.
- Force is a vector quantity.

### Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

- Vector formula: **F**₁₂ = ke × (q1q2/r²) × r̂₁₂
- r̂₁₂ is a unit vector directed from q1 to q2.
- Like charges: q1q2 is positive → force is repulsive.
- Unlike charges: q1q2 is negative → force is attractive.
- Electrical forces obey Newton's Third Law: **F**₂₁ = −**F**₁₂

### Superposition Principle

- The net force on any one charge equals the vector sum of forces from all other charges.
- Forces must be added as vectors.
- Example: **F**₁ = **F**₂₁ + **F**₃₁ + **F**₄₁

### Example 23.1 – Hydrogen Atom

- Electron-proton separation: 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ m
- Electric force: Fe = 8.2 × 10⁻⁸ N
- Gravitational force: Fg = 3.6 × 10⁻⁴⁷ N
- Electric force is vastly larger than gravitational force at atomic scales.

### Example 23.2 – Resultant Force (Right Triangle)

- q1 = q3 = 5.00 μC, q2 = −2.00 μC, a = 0.100 m
- F₂₃ = 8.99 N (attractive, toward q2)
- F₁₃ = 11.2 N (repulsive, at 45°)
- Resultant: **F**₃ = (−1.04 î + 7.94 ĵ) N

### Example 23.3 – Net Force Zero

- q1 = 15.0 μC at x = 2.00 m, q2 = 6.00 μC at origin
- x-coordinate where net force = 0: x = 0.775 m

### Example 23.4 – Charged Spheres in Equilibrium

- Two identical spheres, mass = 3.00 × 10⁻² kg, L = 0.150 m, θ = 5.00°
- Charge magnitude: |q| = 4.42 × 10⁻⁸ C
- Method: balance of tension, gravity, and electric force.

## Topic 6: Electric Field

- The electric field is a field force — it acts through space without physical contact.
- Michael Faraday developed the concept of electric fields.
- An electric field exists in the region around a charged object (source charge).
- A test charge placed in the field experiences an electric force.
- Electric field definition: **E** ≡ **F** / q₀ (force per unit positive test charge)
- **E** is produced by the source charge, independent of the test charge.
- The test charge serves only as a detector of the field.
- Direction of **E**: same as force on a positive test charge.
- SI unit of electric field: N/C
- Electric field of a point charge: **E** = ke × (q/r²) × r̂
- For multiple source charges: **E** = ke × Σ(qi/ri²) × r̂ᵢ
- Relation between force and field: **F**e = q**E**
  - If q is positive: force and field are in the same direction.
  - If q is negative: force and field are in opposite directions.

### Electric Field Direction

- Positive source charge: field lines point away (outward).
- Negative source charge: field lines point toward (inward).

### Example 23.5 – Suspended Water Droplet

- Mass = 3.00 × 10⁻¹² kg, E = 6.00 × 10³ N/C downward
- Droplet is suspended → electric force balances gravity.
- Charge: q = −mg/E = −4.90 × 10⁻¹⁵ C (negative charge)

### Example 23.6 – Electric Field from Two Charges

- q1 (positive) at distance a, q2 (negative) at distance b from origin.
- Point P at (0, y).
- Special case |q1| = |q2| = q, a = b:
  - Ey = 0 (y-components cancel)
  - Ex = 2ke × q × a / (a² + y²)^(3/2)
- For y >> a (far field / electric dipole): E ≈ ke × 2aq / y³

## Topic 7: Electric Field of Continuous Charge Distributions

- When charge spacing << distance to point of interest, treat charge as continuous.
- Volume charge density: ρ = Q/V (units: C/m³)
- Surface charge density: σ = Q/A (units: C/m²)
- Linear charge density: λ = Q/ℓ (units: C/m)

### Example 23.7 – Charged Rod

- Rod of length ℓ, uniform charge λ, total charge Q.
- Point P at distance a from one end along the axis.
- Electric field: E = keQ / [a(ℓ + a)]

### Example 23.8 – Uniform Ring of Charge

- Ring of radius a, total charge Q.
- Point P at distance x from center along axis.
- Electric field: E = (ke × x × Q) / (a² + x²)^(3/2)

### Example 23.9 – Uniformly Charged Disk

- Disk of radius R, surface charge density σ.
- Point P at distance x from center along axis.
- Electric field: Ex = 2πkeσ × [1 − x / (R² + x²)^(1/2)]

## Topic 8: Electric Field Lines

- Field lines are a pictorial tool to represent the electric field.
- The electric field vector **E** is tangent to the field line at each point.
- Direction of field line = direction of electric force on positive test charge.
- Denser field lines = stronger electric field.
- Surface A has denser field lines than B → stronger field at A.

### Rules for Drawing Electric Field Lines

1. Lines begin on positive charges and terminate on negative charges.
2. If excess charge of one type exists, some lines go to/from infinity.
3. Number of lines ∝ magnitude of the charge.
4. No two field lines can cross.
5. Field lines are not physical objects — they are a visualization tool.

### Field Line Patterns

- Single positive charge: lines radiate outward spherically.
- Single negative charge: lines point inward from all directions.
- Electric dipole (+q and −q): lines leave positive, terminate on negative; equal numbers.
- Two equal positive charges: lines repel each other; field ≈ 2q at large distance.
- +2q and −q: two lines leave +2q for each line terminating on −q; field ≈ +q at large distance.

## Topic 9: Motion of a Charged Particle in a Uniform Electric Field

- A charged particle in an electric field experiences: **F**e = q**E** = m**a**
- Acceleration: **a** = q**E**/m
- If **E** is uniform → acceleration is constant → kinematic equations apply.
- Positive charge: acceleration in the direction of **E**.
- Negative charge: acceleration opposite to **E**.
- An electron projected horizontally into a vertical **E** field follows a parabolic path.

### Example 23.10 – Accelerating Positive Charge (Two Models)

- Uniform field **E** between parallel plates separated by distance d.
- Positive charge q, mass m released from rest at plate A.
- Model A (constant acceleration): vf = √(2qEd/m)
- Model B (energy): W = ΔK → Fe × Δx = ½mvf² → vf = √(2qEd/m)
- Both models give the same result.

# CHAPTER 24: GAUSS'S LAW

## Topic 1: Electric Flux

- Electric flux ΦE is the product of the electric field magnitude and the perpendicular surface area.
- Formula: ΦE = EA (when **E** is perpendicular to surface)
- General formula: Φ = EA cos θ (θ = angle between **E** and the normal to the surface)
- Electric flux is proportional to the number of field lines penetrating the surface.
- Maximum flux: surface perpendicular to **E** (θ = 0°).
- Zero flux: surface parallel to **E** (θ = 90°).
- SI unit of electric flux: N·m²/C
- Net flux through a surface = lines leaving − lines entering.

### Example 24.1 – Flux Through a Cube

- Uniform field **E** in the x-direction, cube of edge length ℓ.
- Face 1 (left): **E**·**A** = −Eℓ² (field enters)
- Face 2 (right): **E**·**A** = +Eℓ² (field exits)
- All other faces: **E**·**A** = 0 (field parallel to surface)
- Net flux: ΦE = −Eℓ² + Eℓ² + 0 + 0 + 0 + 0 = 0

## Topic 2: Gauss's Law

- Gauss's law relates the net electric flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge.
- The closed surface used in Gauss's law is called a Gaussian surface.
- The Gaussian surface is a mathematical surface — it need not coincide with a real surface.
- Gauss's law is fundamental in studying electric fields.
- For a point charge, field lines are radially outward and perpendicular to any spherical surface.
- Net flux through a spherical surface around charge q: ΦE = 4πkeq = q/ε₀
- Gauss's Law (general form): ΦE = ∮ **E**·d**A** = qin/ε₀
  - qin = net charge inside the closed surface
  - **E** = total electric field at any point on the surface (from all charges, inside and outside)
- Net flux through a closed surface with no enclosed charge = 0.
- The flux depends only on the charge enclosed, not on the shape of the surface.

### Key Results from Gauss's Law

- If qin is tripled → flux is tripled.
- If the radius of the Gaussian sphere is doubled → flux is unchanged (but field at surface changes).
- If the Gaussian surface changes shape (e.g., sphere to cube) → flux is unchanged.
- If the enclosed charge moves to a different location inside → flux is unchanged.

## Topic 3: Applications of Gauss's Law

### Strategy

- Choose a Gaussian surface where the surface integral simplifies.
- The surface does not need to coincide with a physical surface.

### Spherically Symmetric Charge Distribution (solid sphere, radius a, total charge Q)

- Inside the sphere (r < a): E = ke × (Q/a³) × r (field increases linearly with r)
- Outside the sphere (r > a): E = ke × Q/r² (same as a point charge)

### Field from a Line of Charge (linear charge density λ)

- Gaussian surface: coaxial cylinder of radius r and length ℓ.
- Electric field: E = λ/(2πε₀r) = 2ke × λ/r
- Field decreases as 1/r (not 1/r² like a point charge).

### Field from a Plane of Charge (surface charge density σ)

- Single infinite plane: E = σ/(2ε₀)
- Two parallel plates (capacitor configuration): E = σ/ε₀

## Topic 4: Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

- Electrostatic equilibrium: no net motion of charge within the conductor.
- Properties of a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium:
  1. The electric field is zero everywhere inside the conductor (solid or hollow).
  2. Any net charge resides entirely on the surface.
  3. The electric field just outside the surface is perpendicular to the surface and has magnitude E = σ/ε₀.
  4. Surface charge density is greatest where the radius of curvature is smallest (sharp points).

### Sphere and Shell Example

- Solid insulating sphere of radius a, charge Q surrounded by conducting shell (inner radius b, outer radius c) with charge −2Q.
- Region 1 (r < a): E₁ = ke × (Q/a³) × r
- Region 2 (a < r < b): E₂ = ke × Q/r²
- Region 3 (b < r < c, inside conductor): E₃ = 0
- Region 4 (r > c): E₄ = −ke × Q/r² (net enclosed charge = Q + (−2Q) = −Q)
